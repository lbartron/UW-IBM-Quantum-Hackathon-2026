Quantum Kernels in the Sparse Regime:
Anatomy, Scarcity & Empirical Trade-offs

An empirical investigation into 180-dimensional QPU projections using the ZZFeatureMap.
The Goal: Rigorously identifying the specific data regimes where quantum data encoding demonstrates measurable advantages over classical RBF baselines.

Track 1: Anatomy of the Projection

The Full XYZ Advantage

Inspired by prior works in quantum sensor analytics, we hypothesized that isolating specific measurement bases might mitigate hardware gate noise and artificially improve classifier performance.

The full 180-dim projection includes X, Y, and Z measurements. We sliced the data into its 60-dim orthogonal components to test this hypothesis.

Empirical Result: The hypothesis was rejected. Isolating measurements to the Z-Basis or Y-Basis resulted in the lowest F1 scores (0.649). Retaining the full dimensionality of the XYZ projection provided the optimal informational signal (0.757).

Track 2: Nuance in Data Scarcity

We iteratively stripped training data from 100% down to 10% under locked random seeds to evaluate model degradation in sparse regimes.

  • Regime-Dependent Efficacy: No single model universally dominates; performance hierarchy fundamentally shifts based on data availability.
  • Mid-Sparse Quantum Advantage: The isolated Y-Basis projection demonstrates notable resilience, significantly outperforming both the Classical RBF and other quantum models in the 25% to 50% data windows.
  • Full XYZ Scaling: The complete 180-dim XYZ projection suffers under severe starvation (<25%) due to model capacity, but scales most effectively to achieve the highest peak accuracy at 100% data.

The Limitation: Classical Stability in Noise

Noise Injection Testing

We tested robustness by injecting escalating standard deviations (σ) of Gaussian noise into the testing features.

The finding: The Classical RBF model proves significantly more robust, maintaining a strictly higher absolute accuracy ceiling than the Quantum Z-Basis model across all evaluated noise levels.

Notably, the quantum model exhibits a non-monotonic response to noise—peaking in accuracy at σ=0.10. This indicates that low-level noise acts as a regularizer on the high-dimensional quantum geometry before entanglement amplification induces a steep performance collapse at σ ≥ 0.15.

Summary of Findings

  • ✓
    Targeted Sparse-Regime Resilience While the full projection struggled in extreme scarcity, isolated sub-projections (Y-Basis) successfully out-scaled classical models in the 25%-50% data availability window.
  • ✓
    Scaling Potential with Full Data When provided with the complete dataset (100%), the full XYZ quantum model overcame its low-data variance to achieve the highest overall predictive score.
  • ✗
    Strict Suboptimality in Noisy Environments Despite exhibiting intriguing noise-induced regularization at low σ, the quantum architecture was consistently outperformed by the classical baseline in absolute accuracy across the entire noise spectrum.
