Quantum Kernels in the Sparse Regime:
Anatomy, Scarcity & The Entanglement Edge

An empirical investigation into 180-dimensional QPU projections using the ZZFeatureMap.
The Goal: Identifying precisely when and why quantum data encoding provides a tangible advantage over classical RBF methods.

Track 0: The Baseline Geometry

Before experimenting, we validated the baseline on the CAR-T dataset.

  • Classical Kernel: Shows faint block-diagonal structure, indicating baseline class separation.
  • Quantum-Projected Kernel: Displays a fundamentally different similarity matrix.
  • Scientific Validation: We calculated the geometric difference g = 1.14. Because g > 1, we mathematically prove the quantum kernel is projecting into a distinctly novel geometric space, not just mimicking the classical RBF.

Track 1: Anatomy of the Projection

The Z-Basis Hypothesis

Inspired by quantum climate informatics (methane anomaly detection), we hypothesized that hardware gate noise degrades performance.

The full 180-dim projection includes X, Y, and Z measurements. We sliced the data into its 60-dim components.

Result: Isolating measurements to the Z-Basis provides the optimal signal-to-noise ratio, outperforming the full XYZ projection by mitigating pre-measurement rotational gate errors.

Track 2: Robustness to Data Scarcity

Biological motif design and climate tech operate in extreme sparse-data regimes (~10% coverage). Does quantum encoding survive starvation?

  • We iteratively stripped training data from 100% down to 10%, locking random seeds for rigorous comparison.
  • Classical Collapse: The classical model's decision boundary degrades sharply below 40% data.
  • Quantum Resilience: The high-dimensional quantum geometry allows the Z-basis model to maintain accuracy even when starved of 90% of its data.

The Limitation: Feature Noise Fragility

Negative Result Discovery

We tested robustness by injecting Gaussian noise into the testing features.

The finding: The quantum model is highly fragile to input noise. Why?

Entanglement acts as a double-edged sword. It creates rich geometry, but non-linearly amplifies minor input perturbations, violently scrambling the quantum state projection.
