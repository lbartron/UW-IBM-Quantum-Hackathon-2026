Quantum Kernels in the Sparse Regime:
Anatomy, Scarcity & Classical Superiority

An empirical investigation into 180-dimensional QPU projections using the ZZFeatureMap.
The Goal: Identifying precisely when and why quantum data encoding provides a tangible advantage over classical RBF methods—and when it falls short.

Track 0: The Baseline Geometry

Before experimenting, we validated the baseline on the CAR-T dataset.

  • Classical Kernel: Shows faint block-diagonal structure, indicating baseline class separation.
  • Quantum-Projected Kernel: Displays a fundamentally different similarity matrix.
  • Scientific Validation: We calculated the geometric difference g = 1.14. Because g > 1, we mathematically prove the quantum kernel is projecting into a distinctly novel geometric space, not just mimicking the classical RBF.

Track 1: Anatomy of the Projection

The Full XYZ Advantage

Inspired by quantum climate informatics, we initially hypothesized that isolating specific measurement bases might mitigate hardware gate noise and improve performance.

The full 180-dim projection includes X, Y, and Z measurements. We sliced the data into its 60-dim components to test this.

Result: Contrary to our hypothesis, isolating measurements to the Z-Basis or Y-Basis resulted in the lowest F1 scores (0.649). Retaining the full XYZ projection provided the optimal signal, achieving the highest score of 0.757.

Track 2: Vulnerability to Data Scarcity

Biological motif design and climate tech operate in extreme sparse-data regimes (~10% coverage). Does quantum encoding survive starvation better than classical models?

  • We iteratively stripped training data from 100% down to 10%, locking random seeds for rigorous comparison.
  • Classical Dominance: The classical RBF model consistently outperforms the quantum models across almost all data sparsity levels, maintaining robust accuracy.
  • Quantum Instability: The high-dimensional quantum geometry proves highly erratic. The Z-basis model suffers a dramatic accuracy crash at 25% data availability, disproving our hypothesis of quantum resilience.

The Limitation: Classical Supremacy in Noise

Empirical Discovery

We tested robustness by injecting Gaussian noise into the testing features.

The finding: The classical RBF model consistently maintains a higher absolute accuracy ceiling than the Quantum Z-Basis model across all noise levels.

Interestingly, the quantum model exhibits a slight accuracy increase at low noise injections (σ=0.10). This suggests low-level noise acts as a regularizer for the complex quantum geometry, before entanglement amplification causes a steep collapse at higher levels.
